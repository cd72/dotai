The Immune System: An Overview

The immune system is the body's defence network against pathogens - disease-causing
organisms such as bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites. It is conventionally divided
into two branches that work together: the innate immune system and the adaptive immune
system.

The innate immune system is the body's first responder. It is non-specific, meaning it
attacks any foreign material in the same general way, and it acts within minutes to hours.
Its defences fall into three lines: physical and chemical barriers (the skin, mucous
membranes, stomach acid, and tears), cellular defences (phagocytes such as macrophages and
neutrophils that engulf and digest pathogens), and the inflammatory response. Crucially,
the innate system has no memory: it responds identically every time it meets the same
pathogen.

The adaptive immune system, by contrast, is specific and slower, taking days to mount a
full response on first exposure. Its defining feature is immunological memory - after an
infection it retains long-lived memory cells, so a second encounter with the same pathogen
produces a far faster and stronger response. This is the principle on which vaccination
depends. The adaptive response is carried out chiefly by two classes of lymphocyte: B cells
and T cells. B cells produce antibodies, proteins that bind to specific antigens and mark
them for destruction. T cells come in two main types: helper T cells, which coordinate the
immune response by releasing signalling molecules called cytokines, and cytotoxic T cells,
which directly kill infected body cells.

An antigen is any molecule that the immune system can recognise as foreign and that
triggers an immune response. Antibodies are exquisitely specific: each antibody recognises
just one antigen, fitting it like a key in a lock.

The inflammatory response is a key innate mechanism. It unfolds in a characteristic
sequence: first, damaged tissue and immune cells release chemical signals such as histamine;
second, these signals cause local blood vessels to dilate and become more permeable; third,
increased blood flow brings phagocytes and clotting factors to the site; fourth, phagocytes
engulf pathogens and debris. The visible signs of inflammation are the classic four:
redness, heat, swelling, and pain.

Vaccination exploits immunological memory deliberately. A vaccine introduces a harmless
form of a pathogen - a weakened or killed microbe, or just a fragment such as a surface
protein - so that the adaptive system mounts a response and forms memory cells without the
person suffering the actual disease. On later exposure to the real pathogen, the memory
response neutralises it before illness develops.
