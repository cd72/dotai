# UK Holiday Pay Law — Reference Guide

## Statutory Entitlement

- **Working Time Regulations 1998 (WTR)** give all workers (not just employees) a minimum of **5.6 weeks' paid holiday per year** (28 days for a 5-day week), including bank holidays unless the contract grants them separately.
- Part-time workers accrue holiday **pro rata**.
- The holiday year runs as defined in the contract; if not stated, it defaults to 1 January–31 December.

## Accrual

- Holiday accrues from day one of employment.
- For irregular/zero-hours workers, the commonly used method is **12.07% of hours worked** (though this was challenged in *Harpur Trust v Brazel [2022]*; for workers with no normal working hours, each week of leave must be paid at the **average of the last 52 weeks in which pay was earned** — up to 104 weeks back).
- Fixed-term contracts: full entitlement still applies; no rolled-up holiday pay unless agreed in writing and clearly itemised (and even then it is legally questionable).

## Taking Holiday & Carry-Over

- Workers must be **allowed** to take their leave; employers cannot simply refuse all requests and then claim the worker owes them leave.
- Unused leave can only be carried over if:
  - The employer prevented the worker from taking it (WTR reg 13(9) as interpreted in *King v Sash Window Workshop [2017]*).
  - The worker was on sick leave, maternity/paternity leave, or other statutory leave.
  - The contract expressly permits carry-over.
- **"Use it or lose it" clauses** are only enforceable if the employer actively reminded the worker to take leave and gave them a real opportunity to do so (*Max-Planck v Shimizu [2018]*, ECJ; retained in UK law post-Brexit).

## Holiday Pay Calculation

- Pay during leave must reflect **normal remuneration** — including regular overtime, commission, and other regular payments (*Bear Scotland v Fulton [2014]*; *Flowers v East of England Ambulance Trust [2019]*).
- Basic-pay-only calculations that exclude regular overtime are likely unlawful.
- A series of underpayments can be claimed as an **unlawful deduction from wages** under the Employment Rights Act 1996 (ERA 1996), provided no gap of more than 3 months between deductions.

## Overpaid / Over-Taken Holiday

- If a worker leaves having taken **more holiday than accrued**, the employer **may** deduct the excess from final pay — but **only if the contract expressly allows this** (ERA 1996 s.13; *Hill v Chapell [2003]*).
- If the contract is silent, the employer cannot make a unilateral deduction; they would have to sue in the civil courts.
- Employers cannot recover holiday pay overpayments through a debt recovery agency unless they have a contractual or court-backed right to do so.

## Debt Recovery & Harassment

- A debt recovery firm can only legitimately pursue a debt that is **legally established** — i.e. the employer has a clear contractual right or a court judgment.
- The **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)** regulates debt collectors; aggressive or misleading letters may breach the FCA's Consumer Credit sourcebook (CONC) and the **Protection from Harassment Act 1997**.
- The **Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA)** regulates solicitors; misleading letters from solicitors can be reported.

## Key Limitation Periods

| Claim Type | Limitation |
|---|---|
| Unlawful deduction from wages (ET) | 3 months less a day from last deduction |
| Breach of contract (ET) | 3 months less a day from termination |
| Breach of contract (County Court) | 6 years |
| Holiday pay under WTR | 3 months less a day from date pay was due |

## Employer's Burden of Proof

- The employer must be able to **demonstrate** that more holiday was taken than accrued — this means producing holiday records, payroll data, and a clear calculation.
- If records are incomplete or contradictory, this undermines their claim.
- Workers have the right to request their personal data (including holiday records) under a **Subject Access Request (SAR)** — GDPR / UK Data Protection Act 2018; employer must respond within **one calendar month**.

## Key Cases to Know

- *Harpur Trust v Brazel [2022] UKSC 21* — holiday pay for part-year workers
- *King v Sash Window Workshop [2017]* — carry-over when employer prevents leave
- *Bear Scotland v Fulton [2014]* — overtime must be included in holiday pay
- *Williams v British Airways [2011]* — normal remuneration standard
- *Hill v Chapell [2003]* — contractual authority needed to deduct overtaken leave
